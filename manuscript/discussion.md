# Discussion

This study demonstrates the recovery and characterization of near-complete adenovirus genomes from animal-derived metagenomic sequencing datasets. Through reference-guided assembly, comparative genomics, phylogenetic analysis, and functional variant annotation, two distinct adenovirus genomes were reconstructed and classified as AdV-12-like and AdV-18-like viruses.

Phylogenetic analysis consistently placed the goat-derived genome within the Human adenovirus 12 lineage and the dog-derived genome within the Human adenovirus 18 lineage. These classifications were independently supported by BLAST similarity searches, genome-wide sequence identity, and maximum-likelihood phylogenetic reconstruction. The agreement among these complementary analytical approaches increases confidence in the taxonomic assignment of both genomes.

An important observation is that both recovered genomes clustered within established Human mastadenovirus A diversity rather than with non-human mastadenovirus outgroups. Although the samples originated from animal-associated metagenomic datasets, the detected viral genomes were most closely related to recognized human adenovirus lineages. This finding highlights the complex ecological interfaces that may exist between humans, domestic animals, and environmental reservoirs.

The inclusion of African contextual genomes provided additional epidemiological insight. The goat-derived AdV-12-like genome clustered near a recently reported Kenyan sequence, while the dog-derived AdV-18-like genome grouped closely with Tanzanian genomes. Although the available dataset does not permit inference of transmission pathways, these relationships demonstrate that the recovered genomes are positioned within broader African adenovirus diversity rather than appearing as isolated or highly divergent lineages.

Genome-wide variant analysis revealed extensive sequence divergence relative to reference genomes. Hundreds of coding-region variants were identified in both reconstructed genomes, with variation distributed across multiple viral genes rather than concentrated within a single genomic region. Structural proteins including hexon, fiber, and penton contained substantial numbers of variants. These proteins are among the most biologically important adenovirus components because they contribute to viral assembly, cell entry, tissue tropism, and host immune recognition.

Functional annotation demonstrated that synonymous substitutions outnumbered amino-acid-changing mutations in both genomes. This pattern is consistent with the action of purifying selection, whereby many nucleotide changes accumulate without altering protein sequence while disruptive amino-acid substitutions are removed over evolutionary time. Nevertheless, numerous missense variants were detected across major structural and replication-associated genes, indicating ongoing diversification within these viral lineages.

From a surveillance perspective, the results illustrate the value of metagenomic sequencing for recovering complete or near-complete viral genomes without prior assumptions regarding viral identity. Traditional diagnostic assays often target a limited set of pathogens or genomic regions, whereas metagenomic approaches enable comprehensive detection and characterization of viral diversity. The recovery of two phylogenetically distinct adenovirus genomes from animal-associated samples demonstrates the utility of this strategy for viral discovery and genomic surveillance.

Several limitations should be considered. First, the study analysed a small number of genomes and therefore cannot provide estimates of adenovirus prevalence or transmission dynamics. Second, reference-guided consensus reconstruction may not fully capture highly divergent genomic regions. Third, functional effects inferred from sequence variation are computational predictions and were not experimentally validated. Consequently, the biological consequences of the identified amino acid substitutions remain unknown.

Future work could expand sampling across additional hosts, geographic locations, and time points to investigate adenovirus diversity within a broader One Health framework. Long-read sequencing, de novo assembly approaches, and experimental characterization of key mutations may further improve understanding of adenovirus evolution and host adaptation.

In conclusion, this study recovered and characterized two near-complete adenovirus genomes from animal-derived metagenomic datasets and demonstrated their placement within Human mastadenovirus A diversity. The integration of genome reconstruction, phylogenetics, and functional variant analysis provides a reproducible framework for adenovirus genomic surveillance and highlights the potential of metagenomic approaches for investigating viral diversity at the human–animal interface.
